    Example 38.

    Let \(\Omega\) be a sample space and \(A \subset \Omega\) an event. Let
    \begin{align*} X = \begin{cases} 1 \amp x \in A \\ 0 \amp x \notin A \end{cases} \end{align*}

    Example 39.

    Suppose we flip a coin \(n\) times. Let \(H_i\) indicate heads on flip \(i\text{.}\) Let \(S\) be the total number of heads in all flips. Then:
    \begin{gather*} S = H_1 + H_2 + \dotsb + H_{n} \end{gather*}
    If \(p\) is the probability of the coin coming up heads on a flip, then \(S\) has the binomial distribution with parameters \(n, p\text{.}\) Weâ€™ll use the notation \(S \sim \Bin(n, p)\) and:
    \begin{gather*} b(k) = b(k; n, p) = \Pr(S = k). \end{gather*}
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose the coin has \(p = 0.3\) and we flip it \(n = 4\) times. Find \(b(2) = b(2; 4, 0.3)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The relevant flip sequences are:
    \begin{gather*} THHT, HHTT, TTHH, THTH, HTHT, HTTH \end{gather*}
    Each individual sequence has a probability of \((0.3)(0.3)(0.7)(0.7) = 0.0441\text{.}\) So the total probability is:
    \begin{gather*} b(2; 4, 0.3) = (6)(0.0441) = 0.2646. \end{gather*}
    That is, (number of flip sequences)(probability of each sequence).
